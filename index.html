Lore ▼
Ages 1–6

Born December 17th, one year into the apocalypse, into a family already buckling under the weight of a collapsing world. They put her up for adoption to give her a better shot — and didn't disclose the illnesses running through their bloodline, afraid it would cost her a good home. The Jones family took her in. Stable, reasonably kind, financially intact. She never fit. There was always a silent distance between her and their other children — nothing said, nothing done, just a gap that never closed. When her conditions started showing — slower, more breaks, conversations that took longer to parse — she started working out obsessively to keep pace. She didn't know she was making some of it worse. Burdened with the guilt of dragging everyone down, she ran at six.

Ages 7–16

She found Odette almost immediately — or Odette found her. They became inseparable. Estella never told her about any of it. Not the anemia, not the knees, not the back. She pushed herself to exhaustion every day just to seem fine. She watched the cult take hold of Odette and couldn't figure out how to stop it. Watched her friend take lives and looked away until she couldn't anymore. At 13, two cities away, she tried to end it. The gun jammed. She was furious — couldn't even do that right — and on her way back got jumped by scavengers who took everything except the will to return. She bought a pistol and a shank and walked home. Found Odette. Said nothing. Just before 17, her heart started going wrong. Palpitations. Sharp chest pain. Quiet. Hidden.

Ages 17–19

The same routine, every day: 6am, work out, scavenge, eat, scavenge, Odette, eat, work out, shower, sleep. Monotonous except for the flare-ups — heart, knees, hips, back, anemia, all of it untreated and ongoing. Then at 18 something shifted in Odette. A tension Estella could feel but couldn't name. She tried not to pry. Then Odette ran. Estella's whole world shattered. She searched for a month. When she found her, Odette was different. Estella didn't care. She just needed her back. She still does. She just hasn't said it yet.
